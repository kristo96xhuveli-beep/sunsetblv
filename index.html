Culture

Visit Around

Old Village of Qeparo Perched about 400 metres above the Ionian Sea, the old village core of Upper Qeparo was constructed approximately 1,000 years ago, around 1025 AD, during the late Byzantine period. The village is first mentioned by name in a 1566 letter from the people of Himara seeking refuge from the Ottoman Empire with the Kingdom of Naples. After the fall of communism, most families emigrated, leaving their ancestral homes behind — many of these ancient stone buildings are now in a state of disrepair, giving Qeparo a melancholic sense of faded beauty. Wander-Lush St. Dimitri's Church, Qeparo The Church of St. Demetrius is a white, renovated church dedicated to Saint Demetrius, located in Lower Qeparo. It is one of several historic Orthodox churches in the area that reflect the region's deep-rooted Christian traditions, built primarily in the post-Byzantine style, showcasing frescoes and stone masonry that have endured for centuries. Porto Palermo Castle Porto Palermo Castle was built in 1804 by Ali Pasha of Tepelena, with the help of French military engineers. Even after the Ottoman period, the castle continued to be used, it was occupied by the Italians during World War Two, and later served as part of a submarine base during Albania's communist regime. In 1948 it was declared a Cultural Monument and today serves as a museum. Ruins of Borsh Castle Also known as Sopot Castle, the first habitation at Borsh Castle dates back to the 4th century BC, the time of the Epirote tribe of the Chaonians. Visit Saranda The earliest written documentation dates to the early 13th century, when Archbishop Demetrios Chomatenos wrote about the "archonship of Sopotos". Visit Saranda The castle changed hands many times — from the Venetians to the Ottomans — and was probably abandoned in the early 20th century. Village of Old Himara The cultural monument dates back 3,500 years, when the Greek tribe of the Chaonians inhabited the area. The settlement became fortified from the 8th century BC and was first mentioned in writing by the Roman author Pliny in the first century. The castle was inhabited until the 16th century, when it started to be abandoned in favour of the area downhill near the Port of Himara. Among the ruins are historic churches, including the Church of Saint Sergios and Bakchos, dating to the 11th century, and Saint Michael, dating to the 13th century.
